About the Anion Gap

The anion gap represents unmeasured anions in the serum and is a critical tool in the evaluation of metabolic acidosis. An elevated anion gap suggests the presence of unmeasured anions such as lactate, ketoacids, uremic toxins, or toxic alcohols.

Normal Values

  • Anion Gap: 8-12 mEq/L (without potassium)
  • Albumin-corrected AG: More accurate in hypoalbuminemia
  • Delta-delta ratio: 1-2 (pure AGMA)

Elevated Anion Gap (AGMA) Differential: GOLDMARK

  • G - Glycols (ethylene glycol, propylene glycol)
  • O - Oxoproline (acetaminophen metabolite)
  • L - L-lactate (most common cause)
  • D - D-lactate (short bowel syndrome)
  • M - Methanol
  • A - Aspirin (salicylate toxicity)
  • R - Renal failure (uremia)
  • K - Ketoacidosis (DKA, starvation, alcoholic)

Delta-Delta Ratio Interpretation

  • < 1: Combined AGMA + non-anion gap metabolic acidosis (NAGMA)
  • 1 - 2: Pure anion gap metabolic acidosis
  • > 2: Combined AGMA + metabolic alkalosis (or pre-existing elevated HCO3-)

Formulas

Anion Gap = Na+ - (Cl- + HCO3-) Albumin-Corrected AG = AG + 2.5 × (4.0 - Albumin) Delta-Delta Ratio = (AG - 12) / (24 - HCO3-)
